# Software Engineering Skills Are More Important Than Ever — Overview

## Talk Summary

The talk opens by skewering the recurring "SWE is dead" cycle — headlines from 2022 through 2026 that predict coding's demise on an almost annual schedule — before landing on an honest personal admission: the speaker believed it for a while. That vulnerability sets up the real argument: software engineering is a discipline, not a keyboarding skill, and it rests on two enduring principles that AI makes *more* important, not less — optimize for feedback loops, and manage complexity.

The bulk of the talk is a first-person journey through three stages of AI-assisted development. Stage one ("vibe coding") is a cautionary tale of unconstrained chat-based generation: flailing agents, underspecified prompts, compounding corrections, and eventual abandonment. Stage two (spec-driven development) shows real improvement but reveals a new trap — writing good plans while never actually reading the generated code, which allowed invisible architectural debt to accumulate unseen. Stage three (the "harness") is the synthesis: tooling, sub-agents, and automated review that systematically enforce good design and catch drift before it calcifies, turning the two core principles into self-correcting infrastructure.

The talk closes by returning to the opening fear and resolving it cleanly: the dread was real, but it was aimed at the wrong thing. Typing was never the valuable part. Judgment, design, architecture, and knowing what "good" looks like — those don't get commoditized when the code gets faster to produce. If anything, higher output volume raises the stakes on getting design right.

## As Dragon Warrior Gameplay

If this talk were a Dragon Warrior RPG, the speaker begins the game as a low-level hero who has just read the prophecy scroll: *"The Hero's role is obsolete — the Dragonlord's AI minions will replace all adventurers."* Stage one is wandering into the dungeon without a map, shouting at the party to "just go fight stuff" — the random encounters pile up, the group keeps circling the same room, and eventually everyone dies to a slime. It's the classic experience of burning through all your spell points on the first floor.

Stage two is buying the strategy guide (the spec) and finally knowing where the boss is — but skipping every room description and running straight to the final dungeon without checking your equipment. You have the right plan; you just never looked at your own inventory and find out mid-boss-fight that your sword is cardboard. Stage three is reaching the point where you've built a proper party with roles, auto-battle scripts that correct themselves, and a review companion who flags when someone's casting Fire on a fire-immune enemy. The final boss — "Coding Is Dead" — turns out to be a palette-swapped slime in a trench coat, and the hero's real power was always the judgment to know which floor the treasure was actually on.
